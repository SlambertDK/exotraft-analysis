## 2. History

### 1957 — Birth of an invention
Kai Hermansen hand-built the first chimney fan in Sorø, Denmark, using nothing more than a hand drill and a workshop. The device solved a real problem — poor chimney draught causing smoky, inefficient combustion. Kai patented the invention: it was the world's first chimney fan that could withstand high flue gas temperatures. This wasn't a startup; it was an engineer solving a problem no one else had solved.

The company was named **Exhausto** — a Latin/English portmanteau referencing exhaust and ventilation.

### 1963 — Move to Funen
The company relocated from Sorø to Funen (the island between Jutland and Zealand), eventually establishing permanent headquarters in Langeskov. This remains the home base today, including the development laboratory.

### 1969 — Second generation takes over
After Kai Hermansen's passing, his son **Peter Hermansen** took over in 1969. Peter grew the business substantially — but, as the company's own history notes, "never faster than the organisation and quality of delivery could keep up." This growth discipline is a defining cultural trait that persists today: Exodraft grows carefully, with quality as the governor.

Under Peter's leadership, Exhausto expanded its product range to include industrial fans and ventilation systems alongside chimney fans.

### 1970s–2000s — Building European presence
Through decades of engineering-led product development, Exhausto built deep relationships with chimney sweeps, HVAC installers, and building contractors across Northern Europe. The brand became synonymous with chimney fan reliability. The installer network became a genuine competitive moat — difficult to replicate.

### 2007 — Strategic refocus
In 2007, a pivotal decision: the ventilation division was sold off. Exhausto became laser-focused on two core competencies:
1. **Chimney fans** — for residential, commercial, and industrial applications
2. **Heat recovery** — capturing waste heat from flue gases and industrial processes

This sharpened identity enabled faster product development and deeper expertise in combustion optimization. The name **Exodraft** began to be used, reflecting this new, focused identity (though the legal name transition and full rebrand took additional years).

### 2020–2021 — The Exodraft Group is formed
The company made two significant acquisitions (2020 and 2021), bringing in:
- **Exodraft Energy** — boilers and district heating solutions
- **Exodraft Climate** — heat pumps, cooling, and freezing solutions

These acquisitions transformed Exodraft from a single-product-category specialist into a full-spectrum energy optimization group. The strategic logic: each division solves a piece of the energy efficiency puzzle, and together they offer customers a comprehensive "energy optimization partner" across the building envelope.

This structure also positioned the group for the green transition narrative — district heating integration, heat pump adoption, waste heat recovery, and cleaner combustion forming a coherent portfolio.

### 2021–Present — Global market leader
Exodraft A/S (the chimney fans / particle filters / heat recovery division) operates as the flagship brand of the Exodraft Group. With 67+ years of heritage, a proprietary development laboratory, and a global distribution network, it is the uncontested market leader in chimney fan solutions.

## 9. Strategic Context — Why This Company Interests an AI Innovation Advisor

### 9.1 The paradox of the market leader
Exodraft has earned its position through 67 years of engineering excellence. But market leadership in hardware is increasingly insufficient. The company now faces:
- Commodity pressure from the bottom (Chinese OEM fans)
- Platform pressure from above (smart home ecosystems wanting to own energy devices)
- Data opportunity in the middle (every connected fan = fleet intelligence)

### 9.2 The hardware-to-platform transition
Exodraft sits at a pivotal moment: it has all the ingredients for a platform company (loyal installer base, market coverage, product in every segment) but none of the platform architecture (connected devices, data layer, recurring revenue, AI models trained on combustion data).

The window to build this is roughly **18–36 months** before a better-funded or more AI-native competitor steps into the space.

### 9.3 Regulatory tailwind
Tightening EU and national air quality regulations are creating **forced upgrade cycles** — especially for particle filters. An AI-optimized system that demonstrably reduces particle emissions is both a product and a regulatory compliance tool. This is rare positioning.

### 9.4 The data moat nobody has built yet
No chimney fan company has built a connected fleet with combustion performance data. The company that does first will have:
- Fleet learning (each unit makes the AI model better)
- Switching costs (users don't want to lose their combustion history)
- B2B data value (municipalities, insurers, researchers)

Exodraft has the installed base to seed this flywheel. No one else does.
